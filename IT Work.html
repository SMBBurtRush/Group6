IT Work

Introduction

the IT professional I have interviewed is Steven Coochin (aka DeveloperSteve), who has worked for many different companies and in many different jobs the Steven worked as were developer, developer advocate/evangelist, chief technology officer (CTO). Steven has also travelled 641,444km. Steven did his 100th talk in 2016 at Xero in New Zealand, Xero is a software company that specialises in cloud-based accounting software platform for small and medium sized businesses. Some of the more notable achievements and highlights of Steven’s career include Meeting a variety of coding language, platform and start-up/company founders and creators, working for massive technology brands, working with the United Nations on a global hackathon series, Livestreamed for 9 hours straight with industry guests. Right now, some jobs Steven has worked at are Snyk, PayPal, Xero, BrainTree, IBM, Telstra, Nginx, and the current job Steven is working for, Lumigo. Lumigo is a serverless intelligence platform which helps developers understand and monitor serverless architecture. The Company uses an AI-based engine to show operations inside the application to help start-ups build pure serverless applications and to enable large companies to start migrating existing applications to serverless. Lumigo has an estimate of 62 employees. And two offices, the HQ is in San Fransico, California, U.S.A and the other office is in Tel Aviv-Yafo, Isriel.

What kind of work is done by the IT (Information Technology) professional?

Steven works in Developer relations and has come from a strong background in application development and delivery. The developer relations role is based around on being a company presence amongst industry communities. Helping to promote industry best practice and adoption as a thought leader representing a company or brand.

What kinds of people does the IT professional interact with? Are they other IT professionals? Clients? Investors? The general public?

Developer Relations covers a wide gambit of stakeholder interactions. Within the company I will liaise with almost every department,

  • Product managers and developers, testing new features and giving guidance on product roadmaps.
  • Marketing, advising on tone and audience resonance along with helping create geeky fun and engaging content.
  • Sales, introductions to developers, tech folks and people that need help with plan upgrades.
  • Whole company, submitting talks to conferences and blog/livestream related content creation with product mentions. Additionally building out demos and relevant fun code related projects.
  • General public, sharing knowledge via a variety of mediums.
  • Developer communities, tracking ways to resonate content, understanding latest industry shift along with knowledge sharing and representing company/brand the best way possible.

Where does the IT professional spend most of their time?

Working 100% remotely Steven spends a lot of time interacting with various industry community groups on Twitter, Twitch, YouTube, slack, discord, LinkedIn, VR (Virtual Reality) worlds/platforms and other social platforms.

What aspect of their position is most challenging?

Developer Relations is a multi-hat role and often changes faster than that of most of the tech industry. The most challenging part of the role is juggling all the different aspects to handle day to day plus generating relevant content utilising current best industry practice.

Conclusion

The part that most surprised me was the fact that developer Relations has so many distinct roles they need to do, and how fast the job can change. Having to work with so many different departments would be a challenge with time management and social skills, I don’t think I could ever do that job. Steven mostly works with people in Israel while living in Australia and has a global reach, so the difference in time zones would also be hard. Steven is a hard-working developer with a long and grand career in the industry, working with a lot of various parts from developer to public speaking at conventions, conferences, and meetups. As for what's in the future for Steven we will just have to wait and see.
